## Content
<div>“A Stone More Precious” Notes</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>“A Stone…” read really smoothly and cleverly works against some of the obliqueness which you establish in many of the pieces in <i>Tomb… </i>without diving headlong into the explicit. The piece does what you do well: playful wrangling and wroughting of words, while also serving up introspective blue collar realness, and a very clearly rendered sense of time, place—and specifically—movement and action. Another success of the piece as a whole is the ability to steer clear of any sappy or overly sentimental passages of pathos where it could be so easy to find oneself lurking while engaging in this topic. That is not to say any Heart is missing from the work, but it doesn’t feel like the shove-the-audience’s-nose-into-the-shit intentional and overt manipulation similar to something like Fruitvale Station. In fact, the piece outright turns 180 degrees from that style and it’s in this stroke that your signature obliqueness helps the reader into the real story: the internal lives of The Already Struggling forced to reckon with an unannounced tragedy and how it changes them.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>All of the major events that inform the content of the narrative take place outside of the perspectives presented in the piece; Clarence doesn’t lose his job on-page, the shooting doesn’t take place on-page, etc. Each character’s perspective is relegated to individual iceberg paragraphs, so when we shift from one POV to the next, it’s like the collision of two large external bodies that scrape each other but are unable to reach the core. And to mix the metaphor, this makes each perspective feel like its own planetary system in which the three share some similar vibrations and thoughts and concerns as they orbit round one another, but they also read as entirely separate entities as the gravitational force of the ego reins them back to their own personal center where they can focus on the important thoughts: the ones taking up all the bandwidth, the ongoing ones concerning themselves despite the outside events distracting them from those internal meditations. </div>
