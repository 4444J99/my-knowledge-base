<div>&quotI'll call your brother, but I'll be dead by next Christmas--I can promise that!&quot  The mother yelled across the entirety of the house from the darkest corner of the living room where she often hulked and sulked. She picked up the heaviest phone she ever held, brought it close to her mass, her face full of holes. &quotBet he will wish he had called then.  Bet he will wish he called.  Bet he will.  When I'm dead.&quot Her daughter glared at her from down the hall way, she framed the doorway leading into the kitchen--or rather, the doorway framed her obtusely as the light distorted her silhouette in an alarming way--the overly unbearably yellow kitchen light beaming from the overly unbearable yellow kitchen lightbulb--overly unbearably exposed because their father never finished installing the light fixtures in the house: their father never finished anything, not until it broke anyway. &quotSomeone has to call someone,&quot her daughter said, her form fading further into the overly exposed unbearable yellow light. </div>
