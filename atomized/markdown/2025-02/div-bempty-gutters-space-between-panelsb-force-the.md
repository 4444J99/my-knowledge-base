<div>  •  <b>Empty gutters (space between panels)</b> force the reader to <b>pause, reflect, or assume meaning themselves</b>.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>2. Closure: The Reader’s Role in Comics</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>McCloud introduces <b>one of the most important principles of comics: Closure.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>  <i>“Closure is the phenomenon of observing the parts but perceiving the whole.”</i> </div>
<div><br></div>
<div>In comics, the <b>reader actively fills in the gaps between panels</b>. This is a <b>fundamental difference between comics and film</b>:</div>
<div>  •  In <b>film</b>, everything is shown—there is no ambiguity between cuts.</div>
<div>  •  In <b>comics</b>, the reader <b>mentally connects</b> the missing moments between panels.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>🔥 <b>Example from Understanding Comics</b><br></div>
<div>McCloud shows <b>a single panel of a man raising an axe</b>, then <b>the next panel of a cityscape with a scream written across it</b>. <b>We never see the axe hit—but we KNOW it did.</b> This is closure in action .</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>🔥 <b>Example from The Killing Joke (Alan Moore, Brian Bolland)</b><br></div>
<div>  •  The <b>final scene (Batman and Joker laughing, then silence)</b> leaves the <b>reader to decide whether Batman killed Joker or not</b>.</div>
