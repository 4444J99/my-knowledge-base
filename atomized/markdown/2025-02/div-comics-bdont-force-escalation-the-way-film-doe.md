## Content
<div>  •  Comics <b>don’t force escalation the way film does</b>—instead, they can <b>let the reader linger or rush through moments</b>.</div>
<div>  •  <b>Your job is to design pages that naturally guide pacing.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Activity: Translating Conflict into Comics</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>💡 <b>Take a scene from Whiplash and rewrite it as a comic using both McKee and McCloud’s principles.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Step 1: Choose a Scene from Whiplash</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Example: <b>The “Not My Tempo” scene.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Step 2: Apply McKee’s Story Structure</b><br></div>
<div>  •  <b>Conflict:</b> Andrew is eager to impress Fletcher, but Fletcher humiliates him.</div>
<div>  •  <b>Decision:</b> Andrew <b>pushes himself harder</b> instead of giving up.</div>
<div>  •  <b>Escalation:</b> Fletcher’s abuse <b>gets worse</b> as Andrew <b>tries harder.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Step 3: Apply McCloud’s Visual Principles</b><br></div>
<div>  •  <b>Panel Layout:</b> Use a <b>tight grid to create a sense of control</b> (like in <i>Watchmen</i>).</div>
<div>  •  <b>Closure:</b> Show <b>Fletcher’s raised hand in one panel</b>, and <b>Andrew flinching in the next—but don’t show the actual hit.</b><br></div>
