onments – in these, the development environment is the OS, and it can modify itself (the system is its own metamodel). How does this relate to flattening threads? A recursive MythOS might allow any project to contain sub-projects ad infinitum, each structured the same way, and references between levels remain consistent. For instance, you might have MythOS as a master directory, inside it a project that is itself a mini-MythOS (with its own version history and namespace). This fractal approach ensures uniform naming conventions at every level (the rules repeat), and one can collapse or expand layers as needed. It also resonates with the idea of worlds within worlds (e.g. a simulation that contains a simulation). Hypermedia systems like Ted Nelson’s Project Xanadu had a recursive flavor: documents could include parts of other documents by reference (transclusion), creating a layered tapestry of content. Nelson specifically criticized one-way links and lack of version control on the Web, advocating a system where all content lives in a “docuverse” and documents are composed of bits of other documents, with bidirectional links and version histories intact  .
