<div>Writing is an essential part of a student's experience (and survival) throughout the entirety of their education and beyond, which is why in my composition classes a greater focus is placed on the actual doing over the deliberating. I take a varied approach to the process of writing instruction via instruction focused on process elements, such as prewriting activities to generate ideas, drafting the actual text, and then a cycle of revision and editing. A great weight is placed on the utilization of technology, as mentioned in my previous answers, to support the students and create an environment of productivity followed immediately by an effective method of feedback and assessment tailored to the individual student.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>In my literature courses I insure that the canon is covered as I am a true lover of the canon, but I do not stop there. It is important to me and to my highly diverse students to read works of authors from all over the globe. So while yes, a lot of the early canon leaves out female writers for instance, I believe that it's important to highlight the few that did make it through the annals of history, while also jumping to more modern and contemporary time periods to cover the works of a much more diverse group of authors. World literature spanning from pre-historic texts and classical texts are great places to mine for a diverse reading syllabi of what can be considered canon, while moving into the 1900s we can insure that all races, genders, and orientations can be and should be studied.</div>
