<div>  •  Zeno’s Paradoxes (Domain: Philosophy of Mathematics) – A set of famous paradoxes from ancient Greek philosopher Zeno of Elea, illustrating apparent contradictions in the concept of continuity and infinity. The most famous, Achilles and the Tortoise, argues that a swift hero (Achilles) can never catch a slower tortoise if the tortoise starts ahead, because Achilles must first reach the point where the tortoise began, by which time the tortoise has moved further, and so on ad infinitum. Another, Dichotomy paradox, says one can never reach a destination because one must always cover half the remaining distance first – an infinite number of steps. Resolution/Principle: These paradoxes were resolved much later by the mathematical concepts of convergent infinite series and calculus, showing that an infinite sum of ever-smaller distances can sum to a finite limit. In doing so, they illustrated the principle of infinitesimals and the need for rigor in dealing with infinity. Implications: Zeno’s paradoxes highlight how intuitive reasoning can falter with infinity or continuity.
