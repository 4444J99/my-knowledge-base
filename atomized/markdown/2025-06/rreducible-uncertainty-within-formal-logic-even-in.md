rreducible uncertainty within formal logic – even in a perfectly logical world, not everything is knowable by the system itself. This has analogies in epistemology (limits of knowability) and even in computer science (where it’s related to the halting problem). It’s sometimes cited in discussions about the limits of AI and formal reasoning, highlighting that any sufficiently complex system has intrinsic limits or “blind spots”【40†L608-L616】. The notion that a system cannot contain full knowledge of itself has philosophical echoes (related to the liar’s paradox and self-reference issues).</div>
<div>  •  Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle (Domain: Quantum Physics/Mathematical Physics) – While physical in origin, it’s deeply mathematical. It states that certain pairs of physical properties (like position x and momentum p) cannot both be known to arbitrary precision at the same time. More precisely, the product of the uncertainties (standard deviations) in position and momentum measurements has a lower bound (Δx · Δp ≥ ħ/2). In simple terms: “the position and velocity (momentum) of an object cannot both be measured exactly, at the same time, even in theory”【29†L1-L4】. The more accurately you know one, the less accurately you can know the other【26†L51-L59】. Example: If one tries to pinpoint an electron’s location very precisely, its momentum becomes very indeterminate, and vice versa. This is not a limitation of instruments but a fundamental property of nature arising from wave-particle duality. Implications: In mathematics and logic, this principle has no direct equivalent, but conceptually it places a limit on knowledge within a system.
