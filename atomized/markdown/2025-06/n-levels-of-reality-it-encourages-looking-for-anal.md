<div>  •  Myth of Eternal Return (Domain: Mythology/Religion) – A concept (noted by Mircea Eliade) that traditional cultures often view time as cyclic, with events recurring in patterns, and that rituals often attempt to re-enact primordial events to renew time. The principle is that by returning to the beginning (through ritual or myth), one regenerates the world. Examples: New Year festivals frequently involve themes of chaos and rebirth (e.g., in ancient Babylonia, the chaos dragon Tiamat’s defeat was ritually reenacted to reset cosmic order for the new year). The concept of reincarnation in Indian religions posits an eternal cycle of birth, death, rebirth – life as a recurring law until moksha (liberation) breaks the cycle. Agricultural myths see each spring as the return of life (Persephone returns from Hades, etc.), essentially the same story retold every year. Implications: This idea of cyclical time is a law of cycles in human understanding – contrasted with the linear, historical time concept.
