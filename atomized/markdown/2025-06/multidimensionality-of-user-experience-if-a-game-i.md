<div>  •  Internal Consistency (Worldbuilding Law) (Domain: Fictional World Design) – A general rule that a created fictional world (whether in literature, film, or game) must be internally consistent with its rules and lore. Once rules are established, they should not be arbitrarily violated by the author, or it breaks audience immersion. Examples: If in a fantasy novel it’s stated that vampires cannot cross running water, the author shouldn’t later have a vampire sprint across a river to catch a hero unless a convincing explanation or previously established exception is invoked. In sci-fi, if faster-than-light travel is said to be impossible in the story’s physics, the big climax shouldn’t suddenly have a ship go to ludicrous speed with no justification. Implications: Internal consistency is almost a physical law of fictional universes: it’s the equivalent of conservation laws or causality in the real world. Audiences are quick to spot continuity errors or logical flaws. It doesn’t mean the world’s rules must match reality (they can be fantastical), but they must have logic within that world.
