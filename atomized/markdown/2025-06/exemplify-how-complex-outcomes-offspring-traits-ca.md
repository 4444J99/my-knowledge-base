<div>  •  Hubble’s Law of Cosmic Expansion (Domain: Cosmology) – Deduced by Edwin Hubble in 1929, it states that galaxies are receding from us at speeds proportional to their distance. In essence, the universe is expanding, and the farther away a galaxy is, the faster it appears to be moving away. Example: A galaxy twice as distant as another recedes roughly twice as fast. This is quantified by the Hubble constant (approximately (70 km/s/Mpc) in modern estimates). Implications: Hubble’s law was the first observational evidence for the Big Bang theory – if everything is flying apart now, it implies a common origin where everything was once together. It reflects uniformity (on large scales, every observer sees the same expansion pattern) and connects to the cosmological principle (the universe is homogeneous and isotropic on large scales). Symbolically, the expansion can be seen as a cosmic instance of change and progression over time (sometimes poetically related to the idea of “creation stretching out”).</div>
