<div>  •  Utilitarian Principle (Greatest Happiness Principle) (Domain: Ethics) – The core of utilitarianism, articulated by Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill: an action is right if it tends to promote the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people. In Bentham’s phrasing, it’s about maximizing pleasure and minimizing pain; Mill emphasized quality of pleasures as well. Examples: Policymaking by utilitarian principles would weigh outcomes for overall well-being: e.g. allocating healthcare resources to save the most lives or benefit the most people (though utilitarians also consider intensity of benefit, not just count). Another example is the classic trolley problem: utilitarian reasoning would often favor flipping a switch to save five people at the expense of one (maximizing net lives saved). Implications: Utilitarianism brings a consequentialist calculus to ethics – morality of actions judged by outcomes. It introduces a quantitative, almost law-like approach (Bentham even attempted a “felicific calculus” to sum up utilities).
