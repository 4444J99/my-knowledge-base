<div>  •  Code of Hammurabi (Domain: Ancient Law) – One of the oldest recorded law codes, inscribed by Babylonian King Hammurabi (~1754 BC). It set out rules and corresponding punishments, famously lex talionis, the “eye for an eye” principle【53†L19-L26】 (though the code is much broader). Examples: It covered contract laws, property rights, family law, and set scaled punishments: e.g., if a builder’s negligence caused a house to collapse and kill the owner’s son, then the builder’s son would be put to death – a form of reciprocal justice. If a noble put out the eye of another noble, his eye would be put out; but injuring a commoner had a financial penalty – thus it was not equal across classes. Implications: Hammurabi’s Code represents the principle of retributive justice formalized (“the punishment fits the crime”). It also shows early codification – making law fixed and known, not arbitrary. As a law codex, it’s a precursor to all later legal systems that articulate explicit rules. It reveals a meta-pattern of social contract: in exchange for order, people accept prescribed penalties.
