<div>  •  Magna Carta (1215) (Domain: Constitutional Law) – A charter forced upon King John of England by feudal barons, which for the first time formally limited the power of the monarch and laid groundwork for rights. It established that the king is subject to the law, not above it. Key principles: no freeman shall be deprived of rights or punished except by lawful judgment of peers or by the law of the land (a precursor to due process), and justice should not be sold, denied, or delayed. Examples: Magna Carta’s clause on justice (“To no one will we sell, to no one deny or delay right or justice”) is a direct ancestor of the modern right to a fair trial. Another clause protected the Church from royal interference. While much of it dealt with feudal specifics (like inheritance and feudal payments), the enduring legacy is the principle that even rulers must abide by law and respect certain individual rights. Implications: Magna Carta is often cited as the foundation of rule of law and constitutional law. It’s a symbolic “law” that even kings must obey a higher set of rules.
