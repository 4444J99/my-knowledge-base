<div>  •  Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule) (Domain: Economics/Management) – Observed by Vilfredo Pareto and generalized widely: approximately 80% of consequences come from 20% of causes【52†L1-L8】. In Pareto’s original finding, ~80% of Italy’s land was owned by 20% of the population. This distribution pattern – a few high-impact factors and a long tail of minor ones – recurs in many systems. Examples: In business, often ~20% of customers account for 80% of sales; 20% of software bugs cause 80% of crashes; 20% of patients may consume 80% of healthcare resources. In city populations, a small fraction of cities contain most of the population (Zipf’s law, a related distribution). Implications: The Pareto principle is not a rigid law but a useful heuristic highlighting inequality of distribution (a power-law distribution). It suggests focusing on the “vital few” factors that generate the most results, rather than treating all causes equally. In project management, it guides prioritization (find that critical 20%). It also has social implications: wealth and income tend to distribute unevenly (a few hold a lot, many hold a little). As a cross-domain pattern, it points to scalefree phenomena
