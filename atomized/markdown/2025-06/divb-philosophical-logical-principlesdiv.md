<div>B. [PHILOSOPHICAL / LOGICAL PRINCIPLES]</div>
<div>Structural ideas that govern thought, argument, and metaphysical understanding.</div>
<div>[B1] Law of Non-Contradiction – A statement cannot be both true and false in the same sense.   [B2] Law of the Excluded Middle – A thing is either true or not true—no third state.   [B3] Ockham’s Razor – Simpler explanations are generally better.   [B4] Gödel’s Incompleteness Theorems – No system can prove all truths about itself.   [B5] Dialectical Principle – Truth arises through conflict and synthesis (thesis-antithesis-synthesis).   [B6] Phenomenology – Experience defines reality; subjectivity is the first truth.   [B7] The Hermeneutic Circle – Understanding the whole depends on understanding the parts, and vice versa.</div>
<div>C. [INFORMATION / COMPUTATIONAL LAWS]</div>
<div>Digital, algorithmic, and structural insights from systems and code.</div>
<div>[C1] Church–Turing Principle – All computation can be expressed algorithmically.   [C2] Moore’s Law – Processing power doubles roughly every two years (not eternal, but cultural myth).   [C3] Law of Requisite Variety – A system must be as complex as its environment to survive.   [C4] Conway’s Law – System design reflects the communication structure of the team that built it.   [C5] Simulation Hypothesis – Reality might be computable or emulated.   [C6] Information Wants to Be Free (Stewart Brand) – Info resists containment.   [C7] Feedback Loops – Systems self-correct or self-destruct via internal response mechanisms.</div>
