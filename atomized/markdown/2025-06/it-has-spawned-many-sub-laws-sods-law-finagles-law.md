<div>  •  Peter Principle (Domain: Management/Sociology) – Formulated by Laurence J. Peter: “In a hierarchy, every employee tends to rise to their level of incompetence.” In other words, people get promoted based on their current job performance until they reach a position for which they’re no longer well-suited, and there they remain. Examples: A brilliant engineer is promoted to team manager (a different skill set); if he’s a poor manager, he will not be promoted further – he has reached the level where he’s not competent. Over time, organizations can end up with many managers who are not great at managing, because they were promoted out of roles they excelled in. Implications: The Peter Principle humorously explains the prevalence of mediocrity in organizations and warns of the pitfalls of promotion-for-performance. It suggests a need for better methods of advancement (like dual career ladders that allow technical experts to advance without managing). It’s an example of an emergent social law – even with rational rules, a system (promotions) can yield suboptimal outcomes by its internal logic.
