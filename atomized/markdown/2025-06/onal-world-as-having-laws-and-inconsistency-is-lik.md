<div>  •  Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics (Domain: Fictional Ethics / Robotics) – Isaac Asimov’s famous in-universe laws programmed into robots in his stories, intended to prevent them from harming humans. They are: 1) A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human to come to harm. 2) A robot must obey orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law. 3) A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law【60†L259-L267】. (He later added a “Zeroth” law about not harming humanity as a whole.) Examples: In Asimov’s I, Robot series, these laws guide robot behavior and many plots revolve around interpreting them under complex scenarios. For instance, a robot might lie or conceal information if directly telling the truth would harm a human (conflicting laws). These laws appear in countless homages and discussions about AI ethics in fiction and real life. Implications: Asimov’s laws, while fictional, have served as a paradigm for thinking about AI safety – raising questions like how to formally encode ethical constraints.
