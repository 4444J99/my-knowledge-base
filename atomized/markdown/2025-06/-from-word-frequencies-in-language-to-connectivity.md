<div>  •  Murphy’s Law (Domain: Engineering/Folklore) – A tongue-in-cheek adage: “Anything that can go wrong, will go wrong.”【53†L1-L4】 Often appended with “…and at the worst possible time.” Originating in engineering contexts (attributed to Edward A. Murphy Jr., 1940s) as a wry safety reminder, it’s become a universal pessimistic principle. Examples: If you drop a piece of toast, it tends to land buttered-side down. If you’re in a rush, that’s when you’ll hit every red light. In tech: the moment you demonstrate something to your boss is exactly when it fails. Implications: While not a scientific law, Murphy’s Law captures the intuition of entropy creeping into human affairs – it’s basically a humorous corollary of the second law of thermodynamics for daily life (disorder and mistakes naturally increase). In engineering, it underpins the design ethos of fail-safes and accounting for worst-case scenarios (because if something can fail, assume it will eventually). Psychologically, it might be confirmation bias (we remember the failures at bad times more), but it’s become a cultural principle of caution.
