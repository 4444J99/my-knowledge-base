<div>  •  Newton’s Second Law of Motion (Domain: Physics) – The acceleration of an object is proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. In modern form, it says the time-rate of change of momentum equals the applied force【8†L23-L27】 (often written as $F = ma$ for constant mass). Examples: Pushing an empty shopping cart (low mass) produces a larger acceleration than pushing a loaded cart with the same force. Implications: This law quantifies causality in motion – heavier objects (more inertia) are less affected by the same force, illustrating the relationship between cause (force) and effect (acceleration).</div>
<div>  •  Newton’s Third Law of Motion (Domain: Physics) – For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. In Newton’s words: “to every action, there is always opposed an equal reaction; or, the mutual actions of two bodies upon each other are always equal and directed to contrary [opposite]”【9†L13-L21】. Examples: When you jump, your legs push downward on the ground (action) and the ground pushes you up with an equal force (reaction). A rocket expels gas downward and is propelled upward by the equal-and-opposite thrust. Implications: Emphasizes symmetry and balance in interactions; often generalized metaphorically – e.g. in social dynamics (“actions have consequences”) – though here it’s a precise physical law of forces.</div>
