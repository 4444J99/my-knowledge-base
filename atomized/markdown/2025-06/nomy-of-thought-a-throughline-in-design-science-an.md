<div>  •  Principle of Sufficient Reason (PSR) (Domain: Metaphysics) – Classical formulation by Leibniz (and earlier by Spinoza): “nothing is without a ground or reason why it is.” In other words, for anything that exists or any event that occurs, there must be an explanation or sufficient reason for why it is so and not otherwise【50†L120-L127】. Examples: If a particular substance has a certain property, PSR demands there is an explanation (perhaps in terms of its molecular structure or causal history). On the grand scale, Leibniz used PSR to argue for the existence of God: if the universe exists, there must be a reason (which he posited as God, to avoid an infinite regress of explanations). Implications: PSR is a causality and intelligibility principle – it assumes the cosmos is ultimately rationally understandable and not arbitrary. It underlies scientific inquiry (we assume events have explanations), though in modern physics some question if quantum events have “reasons” or are truly probabilistic.
