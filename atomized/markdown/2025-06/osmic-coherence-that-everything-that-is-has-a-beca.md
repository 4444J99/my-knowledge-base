<div>  •  The Categorical Imperative (Domain: Ethics) – Immanuel Kant’s foundational ethical principle. In its first formulation: “Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.”【47†L21-L24】. This means one should only act in a way that they could rationally want everyone to act in similar circumstances – one’s action should be fit to be a universal rule. Examples: If I consider lying to get out of trouble, I must ask: could I will a universal law that “everyone may lie to avoid trouble”? Such a world would undermine the very concept of truth-telling and trust, thus the maxim fails the test – so lying in that case is morally impermissible by the categorical imperative. Another formulation is to treat humanity always as an end, never merely as a means – meaning people should never be used simply as instruments for one’s own purposes. Implications: The categorical imperative introduces universality and duty to ethics. It’s a rule of moral reasoning requiring consistency and respect for persons.
