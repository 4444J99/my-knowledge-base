<div>  •  Rule of Thirds (Visual Composition) (Domain: Visual Arts/Photography) – A guideline for framing compositions: divide an image into thirds both horizontally and vertically (creating a 3x3 grid), and place important elements along these lines or their intersections rather than centered. This often creates more tension, energy, and interest than centering the subject. Examples: In a photograph, instead of putting the horizon smack in the middle, a photographer will often put it along the upper or lower third-line; a subject’s eyes might fall near one of the upper intersection points rather than dead center. Landscape painters since the 18th/19th century implicitly followed something like this rule to balance sky and land. Implications: The rule of thirds is an aesthetic “law” born of perceptual psychology – our eyes and brain find asymmetry and division pleasing in many cases, probably because it’s a good balance between unity and variety. It’s taught universally in design and photography as a starting composition principle (though of course can be broken for effect). It highlights the pattern of guideline vs. strict rule in art: while not absolute, it’s a reliable approach.
