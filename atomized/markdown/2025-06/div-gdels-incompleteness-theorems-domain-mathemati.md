<div>  •  Gödel’s Incompleteness Theorems (Domain: Mathematical Logic) – Discovered by Kurt Gödel in 1931, these theorems reveal fundamental limits of formal axiomatic systems (like arithmetic). First Incompleteness Theorem: Any consistent formal system that is capable of expressing basic arithmetic cannot be complete – there will exist true statements in the system’s language that cannot be proved within the system【40†L608-L616】. In other words, no such system can prove all truths about the natural numbers. Second Incompleteness Theorem: Such a system cannot prove its own consistency (assuming it is consistent). Example: In Peano arithmetic (a formal system for the natural numbers), Gödel constructed a specific statement roughly equivalent to “This statement is not provable in this system.” If the system could prove that statement, it would be a contradiction; therefore if the system is consistent, it cannot prove it – yet if the system is indeed consistent, the statement is true. This unprovable-but-true statement exemplifies the first theorem. Implications: Gödel’s theorems shook the belief that mathematics could be axiomatized to answer every question.
