<div>  •  Cartesian Doubt (Methodic Doubt) (Domain: Epistemology) – René Descartes’ methodological principle of doubting any knowledge that can possibly be doubted, to arrive at certain foundational truth. By stripping away all uncertain beliefs, Descartes arrived at the famous indubitable axiom “Cogito, ergo sum” (“I think, therefore I am”). Examples: If you doubt the evidence of your senses (they might deceive you), doubt mathematical truths (an evil demon could be tricking you), doubt even your own body’s existence – the one thing you cannot logically doubt is the existence of your own thinking mind (for the act of doubting confirms a doubter exists). This extreme skepticism tests which beliefs survive. Implications: Cartesian doubt established the axiomatic self as a foundation for knowledge, influencing modern philosophy’s focus on epistemic justification. It’s essentially a purification principle: only beliefs that can withstand rigorous doubt qualify as certain. This is analogous to debugging a program by questioning every part, or to science by testing every assumption.
