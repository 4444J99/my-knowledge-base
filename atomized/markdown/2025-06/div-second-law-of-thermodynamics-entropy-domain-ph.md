<div>  •  Second Law of Thermodynamics (Entropy) (Domain: Physics) – In any closed system, entropy (disorder or energy unavailable for work) tends to increase. Equivalently, heat will not flow spontaneously from cold to hot, and no process is 100% efficient. One formulation: the entropy of an isolated system cannot decrease; the system evolves toward thermodynamic equilibrium (maximum entropy)【14†L53-L57】. Examples: An ice cube in a warm room melts (orderly crystal structure disordered into liquid water) and the overall entropy increases. Your coffee cools down because heat disperses into the room, never spontaneously concentrating back into the cup. Implications: This law introduces the arrow of time – a direction to natural processes (things naturally move from order to disorder). It has metaphorical reach: entropy is often used as a metaphor for decay or increasing chaos in social systems, and the need for energy input to maintain order (as in living organisms maintaining structure). It also relates to information theory (disorder as information entropy) and even existential discussions about inevitable decay.</div>
<div>  •  Periodic Law (Periodic Table) (Domain: Chemistry) – When chemical elements are arranged by increasing atomic number, their properties show a recurring periodic pattern. In Dmitri Mendeleev’s formulation: “the elements arranged in order of their atomic numbers [show] an approximate recurrence of their properties”【11†L529-L537】. Examples: Every row of the periodic table marks a repeat of similar chemical behaviors: e.g. the noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, etc.) are all unreactive; alkali metals (Li, Na, K, etc.) all have similar reactive characteristics. This periodicity let Mendeleev predict elements that were not yet discovered by spotting gaps in the table【11†L543-L551】. Implications: The periodic law highlights cyclic patterns and recurrence in nature – a concept that things which seem different can follow underlying cycles. It also underscores the emergence of complex properties from simple numerical order (atomic number), linking the abstract (number of protons) to the tangible world of chemical behavior.</div>
