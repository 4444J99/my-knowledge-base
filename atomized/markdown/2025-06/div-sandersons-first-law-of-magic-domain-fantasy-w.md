<div>  •  Sanderson’s First Law of Magic (Domain: Fantasy Worldbuilding) – Formulated by fantasy author Brandon Sanderson: “An author’s ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic.”【58†L1-L9】. In essence, clarity of magic rules = ability to use magic for plot resolution. If magic is soft/vague, it should not solve major conflicts unexpectedly (else it feels like a deus ex machina). Examples: In Sanderson’s own work (Mistborn), the magic system (ingesting metals to gain powers) is clearly explained and has limitations; thus, he can have clever magic-based solutions to problems because the reader can anticipate or at least accept them. Conversely, Tolkien’s Gandalf has many powers but they’re not explicitly defined; thus, key plot resolutions in Lord of the Rings don’t hinge on suddenly revealing a new spell – instead they come from character choices and non-magic means (e.g., Frodo’s personal journey, Gollum’s intervention). Implications: This “law” has become influential for fantasy writers designing magic systems.
