<div>  •  Yin and Yang (Domain: Eastern Philosophy/Symbolic) – A concept from Chinese philosophy (Taoism) that describes how seemingly opposite or contrary forces are interconnected and interdependent in the natural world, and how they give rise to each other in turn. Yin is passive, dark, receptive, feminine; Yang is active, light, assertive, masculine – but each contains a seed of the other, as depicted in the classic black-and-white circular symbol. Examples: Day (yang) and night (yin) cycle and each contains the emergence of the other (dawn is yin within yang; twilight is yang within yin). In human terms, activity vs rest, or strength vs flexibility – true effectiveness might require a balance (a strong warrior also needs calm focus). Implications: Yin-Yang illustrates dualism as unity – duality not in opposition but in complementarity. It’s a principle that balance and harmony arise from the equilibrium of opposites, and extremes transform into each other. Symbolically, it’s applied widely: diets balancing hot (yang) and cold (yin) foods, medicine balancing energies, even personality traits (soft-spoken people having an inner strength, etc.).
