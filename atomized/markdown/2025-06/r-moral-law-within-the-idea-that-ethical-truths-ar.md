<div>  •  The Golden Rule (Ethic of Reciprocity) (Domain: Ethics/Religion) – A timeless moral maxim found in many cultures and religions: “Treat others as you wish to be treated.”【48†L27-L33】. It’s often phrased in the positive (as above) or negative (“Do not do to others what you would not want done to you.”). Examples: Nearly every major religion has a version: Christianity – “Do unto others as you would have them do unto you”; Confucianism – “Do not impose on others what you do not wish for yourself”; Islam – “Love for your brother what you love for yourself.” In practice, if you wouldn’t want to be cheated, don’t cheat others; if you appreciate kindness, show kindness. Implications: The Golden Rule encapsulates empathy and universality in ethics. It’s a simpler, sentiment-based cousin to Kant’s categorical imperative. It implies a form of moral symmetry or duality – self and other are interchangeable in the eye of ethics. Symbolically, it represents the idea of reciprocity as a guiding principle in social behavior.
