## Prompt:
which languages uses different characters than english/romantic languages?

## Response:
English and the Romance languages use the Latin alphabet. Outside that family, many languages employ fundamentally different writing systems, not just accented variants. The clean way to think about this is by script families rather than by individual languages.

Semitic abjads include Arabic and Hebrew, along with related scripts such as Syriac and Thaana. These primarily record consonants, with vowels optional or secondary.

Indic abugidas descend from Brahmi and include Devanagari used for Hindi, Marathi, and Nepali; Bengali script for Bangla; Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, and Malayalam; Gujarati; Gurmukhi for Punjabi; and Sinhala. Characters represent consonant–vowel units rather than isolated letters.

East Asian logographic and morphosyllabic systems include Chinese Han characters, used for Mandarin and other Sinitic languages, and adapted as Kanji in Japanese. Japanese additionally uses the syllabaries Hiragana and Katakana, which are distinct character sets from Latin.

Korean uses Hangul, a featural alphabet designed around articulatory phonetics. Its characters are visually and structurally unrelated to Latin.

Cyrillic scripts are used by Russian, Ukrainian, Bulgarian, Serbian, Macedonian, and others. While alphabetic, the character set is distinct from Latin and has different historical roots.

Greek uses its own alphabet, historically influential but visually and functionally separate from Latin in modern usage.

Armenian and Georgian each use unique, self-contained alphabets with no Latin derivation.

Ethiopic (Geʽez) is an abugida used for Amharic, Tigrinya, and related languages, with a character system unlike European scripts.

Southeast Asian scripts include Thai, Lao, Khmer, and Burmese, which are Brahmic-derived but visually and structurally distinct from Indic and Latin systems.

Mongolian (traditional vertical script), Tibetan, and various Central Asian scripts also employ non-Latin character systems.

Finally, there are specialized or minority scripts such as Cherokee (a syllabary), Inuktitut syllabics, Tifinagh (used for Tamazight), and N’Ko (used in West Africa), all of which use characters entirely unrelated to English or Romance writing systems.
